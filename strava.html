Analysing Strava running data in Python

üéØ Goal and Research Questions

The goal of this project was to analyze personal running activity data to understand trends, performance improvements, and training patterns over time. Key research questions included:

  • How has the total distance and frequency of runs changed over time?
  • Has my average running pace improved, and what trends can be observed in pace and heart rate?
  • How do variables like distance, elapsed time, and heart rate relate to one another?
  • Are there noticeable outliers or anomalies in performance, and what might explain them?

üìä Data Sources

The dataset comes from personal activity tracking and contains 214 entries across 94 columns. For this analysis, a subset of meaningful variables was used, including:

  • Activity date and type
  • Elapsed time and moving time
  • Distance and average pace
  • Average and maximum heart rate
  • Calories and total steps

All data was imported in CSV format and loaded into Python for cleaning, transformation, and analysis.

üîÑ Process (ETL, Transformations, Exploration)

üîß ETL and Transformations

  • Selected only relevant columns for running activities.
  • Handled missing values: calories were imputed based on similar distances; rows with missing average heart rate were dropped.
  • Converted dates to datetime objects and extracted day, month, year, and time.
  • Converted elapsed and moving times to minutes and calculated average pace (moving time / distance).
  • Detected and noted outliers, particularly in average pace, corresponding to early walk/run sessions.

üîç Exploratory Data Analysis

  • Visualized pairwise relationships between distance, average pace, heart rate, and elapsed time.
  • Examined correlations: faster paces corresponded with higher heart rates; longer distances led to longer elapsed times.
  • Tracked total distance per month and year to identify trends in training volume.
  • Calculated a 10-run rolling average of pace to smooth fluctuations and detect long-term improvements.

üìà Results and Findings

  • Average run duration was ~43 minutes, covering ~5.7 km at a pace of ~7.5 min/km.
  • Most runs ranged between 3.8‚Äì6.3 km, with heart rate averaging 145 bpm and peaks up to 168 bpm.
  • Step counts ranged around 6,800 per run, reaching >26,000 during long sessions.
  • Total running distance increased significantly from 2024 to early 2025, with March 2025 being the highest month (~100+ km).
  • Rolling average pace improved over time, dropping from ~10 min/km in early sessions to ~7 min/km by early 2025, indicating endurance and performance improvements.
  • Temporary increases in pace occurred during longer runs (half-marathon preparation), reflecting strategic low-intensity training.
  • Outliers in pace were explained by early walk/run sessions and training adaptations.

‚ö†Ô∏è Limitations and Future Work

    Limitations:

  • Dataset only reflects personal running activity, limiting generalizability.
  • Some data points were missing (e.g., calories, heart rate), requiring imputation or removal.
  • Analysis does not incorporate external factors such as weather, terrain, or fatigue levels.

    Future Work:

  • Include additional metrics such as heart rate zones, elevation gain, or running surface
  • Apply predictive models to estimate performance improvement or injury risk
  • Visualize seasonal and weekly training patterns; compare running efficiency and pace progression relative to training interventions (e.g., zone 2 workouts, long runs).
